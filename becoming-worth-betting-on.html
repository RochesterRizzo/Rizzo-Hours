Start Here

Students often want a checklist: the right major, the right internship, the right LinkedIn polish, the right campus activities, the right résumé words. Those things matter, but they are not enough.

The deeper question is whether you are developing the traits that make adults, employers, mentors, and colleagues willing to invest in you.

Core claim: The best preparation for careers, graduate school, leadership, friendship, and adult life is character plus competence. One without the other is fragile.

The links and notes below are not assigned readings. They are prompts. Pick one, read seriously, and ask what it would mean to live differently if the argument is right.

Core Principles

1. Become hard to replace.
AI will make routine symbolic work cheaper. The response is not panic. The response is to develop judgment, taste, tenacity, communication, and the ability to use tools rather than be displaced by them.
2. Stop living by checklists.
Requirements matter, but requirements are minimums. Being excellent requires initiative, persistence, curiosity, and an appetite for hard problems.
3. Learn to work with things, people, and symbols.
Many elite students are trained mostly for symbolic work: writing, calculating, analyzing, coding, modeling. The most robust careers often combine symbolic work with people, operations, institutions, technical tools, or physical reality.
4. Work where incentives teach you.
Your first jobs shape your standards. Do not work for people you do not want to become.
5. Be serious about something.
Seriousness orders priorities. If nothing is sacred, life becomes drift.

Character, Jobs, Success

Start Here · Character · Labor Market

A reminder that students anxious to check boxes and get requirements done often miss what employers and institutions actually value.

Occupational personality requirements can be grouped into intellectual tenacity and social adjustment. Intellectual tenacity includes achievement, effort, persistence, initiative, analytical thinking, innovation, and independence. Social adjustment includes emotion regulation, concern for others, social orientation, cooperation, and stress tolerance.

The point is not that skills do not matter. The point is that skills without tenacity, steadiness, cooperation, and judgment are less valuable than students often imagine.

How Do Elite College Graduates Prepare for a Labor Market in a World of AI?

AI and Work · Symbolic Labor · Automation

Remember the three types of jobs: working with things, working with people, and working with symbols. College often prepares students for symbolic work, but AI is changing the economics of routine symbolic tasks.

From now on, all symbol-using jobs involve software development. Instead of writing jobs, there will be jobs developing software that writes. Instead of financial analysis jobs, there will be jobs developing software to do financial analysis.

The practical advice: adopt the software-development mindset. Whenever you catch yourself doing something repetitive, ask whether a tool, script, spreadsheet, template, or system should do it.

Read Arnold Kling

What It Means to Be an Adult

Character and Adulthood · Responsibility

Adulthood is not age. It is responsibility, self-command, honesty, the ability to delay gratification, and the capacity to take care of yourself and others.

  • Take primary responsibility for your actions and choices.
  • Admit mistakes and learn from them.
  • Do not let emotional reasoning and snap judgment run your life.
  • Recognize that the world does not exist to make you happy.
  • Keep promises, act honestly, and value integrity.
  • Develop comfortable control of your own identity.

Surely You Can Be Serious

Character and Adulthood · Seriousness · Priorities

Seriousness is not a late-life reward for playing the game well. It is a basic practice that keeps life from disintegrating.

Being serious means holding something sacred. If you do not feel like you are doing that right now, take a small but perceptible step toward doing that tomorrow, and then another step after that.

Work in a Sociotropic Environment

Where to Work · Incentives · Organizations

Kevin Kelly’s rule is short and brutal: do not ever work for someone you do not want to become.

Work for a profit. Profit-seeking firms are generally more sociotropic: they must create value for customers, and they must develop employees who can help satisfy those customers.

The larger lesson: the organization you join will teach you. Choose carefully.
