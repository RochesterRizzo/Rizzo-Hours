Notes on transition rules

Calculus classes are pre-major requirements and are not subject to overlap requirements. Statistics classes will be subject to standard overlap rules. In special cases, ECON 232 may substitute for ECON 230. Class of 2027, 2028 and 2029 students who have already declared the BA in Econ or Financial Economics may continue in those degree paths. All Class of 2030 students and current undeclared underclassmen will choose from our three new major offerings.

What is Financial Economics?

Financial economics is not simply “business finance.” It is economics applied to risk, time, uncertainty, information, assets, and institutions.

Financial Economics at Rochester

Financial economics is the application of rigorous economic theory to the behavior of financial markets, assets, and institutions. It asks the questions economists have always asked: how do agents make decisions under scarcity and uncertainty, how do prices aggregate dispersed information, and how do incentives shape behavior? It then directs those questions toward some of the most data-rich and consequential terrain in social science: the pricing of risk, the structure of capital, and the movement of money through time.

Few fields in economics can match financial economics for the density of its theoretical achievements and the directness with which those achievements changed the world. Expected utility theory, portfolio theory, the Capital Asset Pricing Model, the Efficient Market Hypothesis, options pricing, and the Black-Scholes model were not merely academic contributions. They were deployed, tested against real markets, and repeatedly disciplined by real-world evidence and real money.

What makes financial economics distinctively economics rather than merely finance is that its methods and ambitions remain those of the economist: building models from first principles, deriving testable implications, and asking what the results reveal about human behavior and market structure broadly.

Rochester’s BS in Financial Economics takes that intellectual tradition seriously. The degree adds corporate finance, investments, accounting, and money and modern financial institutions to a common economics core, while allowing students to build toward quantitative modeling, institutional and regulatory analysis, financial econometrics, data science, AI, machine learning, fintech, crypto, options pricing, international finance, and related areas.

The signal to the world is not simply that a student has learned some finance vocabulary. Done right, financial economics signals the ability to build a model, stress-test an argument, compare counterfactuals, reason under uncertainty, and understand markets as institutions shaped by information, incentives, and rules.

Examples of Economics Faculty Research

Use this as a navigable introduction to what economists at Rochester actually study.

Rochester economics is research with consequence

Rochester faculty publish at the highest levels of economics and work on questions that shape scholarship, policy, and practice: trade and tariffs, labor markets, work from home, education, health, environmental exposure, voting and information aggregation, taxation, macroeconomics, financial econometrics, public economics, and the institutions that govern opportunity.

Alessandria Bils Bagga Figlio Hill Kahn Parro Sleet Vrioni Singleton Barelli Crema Chaumont Kochov
George Alessandria — Global trade shocks, tariffs, and supply chains

International TradeMacroeconomics

  • Studies global trade shocks and how disruptions reshape global supply chains.
  • Recent work on the “tariff tax cut” asks what happens when tariff revenue is used for fiscal reform rather than simple transfers.
  • The key economic question is not merely whether tariffs are costly, but how revenues are used and how other countries respond.
Mark Bils — Macro, prices, welfare, population, and measurement

MacroeconomicsMeasurement

  • Bridges micro- and macroeconomics by asking what lies underneath aggregate trends.
  • Research examines price and wage adjustment, new goods, inflation measurement, leisure technology, and attachment to the workforce.
  • Recent work on population and welfare argues that a rigid focus on GDP per capita can miss large human welfare gains from more people living decent lives.
Sadhika Bagga — Work from home and the labor market

LaborMacroeconomics

  • Studies how COVID-era remote work reshaped quitting, job changing, hiring, and wage patterns.
  • Work from home became valuable enough that workers may accept lower pay for the flexibility.
  • Students entering the labor market need to understand how amenities, flexibility, and benefits change compensation and career paths.
David Figlio — Education, health, social policy, and human capital

EducationHuman Capital

  • Works on how education, health, and social policy shape long-term human capital development.
  • Research has studied immigrant peer exposure, school report cards and housing markets, early health conditions, and later educational outcomes.
  • His work is a model of how applied economics can connect careful identification to policy-relevant questions.
Elaine Hill — Health, environment, inequality, and exposure

Environmental EconomicsHealth Economics

  • Studies how environmental exposure and energy development affect health, housing, pregnancy, opioids, and inequality.
  • Work in the HEEL lab has examined fracking, infant health, asthma, water quality, opioids, overdose, COVID, long COVID, rural residents, and racial minorities.
  • This is economics as empirical public health, environmental policy, and distributional analysis.
Lisa Kahn — Recessions, skills, careers, and the future of work

LaborCareers

  • Studies how recessions, COVID-19, and technological change reshape jobs, skills, wages, and the value of education.
  • Research on graduating during a recession shows large initial earnings losses that persist over time.
  • Her work helps students think seriously about recession-proofing, skills, job postings, education demands, and changing labor-market requirements.
Fernando Parro — International trade and the China shock

International TradeGeneral Equilibrium

  • Specialist in international trade and general-equilibrium modeling.
  • Work on the China trade shock uses dynamic models across states and sectors to quantify manufacturing job losses and welfare gains from lower-cost imports.
  • This research illustrates the central economic discipline of weighing gains, losses, adjustment, and counterfactuals at the same time.
Christopher Sleet — Macroeconomics, public economics, and optimal taxation

Public EconomicsMacro Theory

  • Works on macroeconomics, public economics, tax policy design, computational methods, and dynamic contract theory.
  • Research on optimal taxation of income-generating choice models workers as choosing bundles of attributes: sector, hours, location, gig/full-time status, rural/urban, and more.
  • The policy implication is that tax systems should account for the actual margins people use when choosing how to make a living.
Iris Vrioni — Education, bias, health access, and institutional design

EducationHealth

  • Uses real administrative and experimental data to show how design, bias, and cost shape education and health outcomes.
  • Research on math competitions examines how evaluators misjudge talent even when performance is comparable.
  • Other work studies contraception cost-sharing, take-up, method choice, unwanted pregnancies, birth rates, and projected savings.
John Singleton — School boards, charter schools, funding formulas, and access

EducationPublic Economics

  • Studies how institutional rules and policy levers reshape school access, equity, and outcomes.
  • Research on school boards uses ballot-order variation to show how board composition changes policy.
  • Work on charter-school funding examines how cost-adjusted funding can affect charter supply, equity, and effectiveness.
Paulo Barelli — Game theory, elections, and information aggregation

Game TheoryPolitical Economy

  • Works in economic theory and game theory.
  • Recent work asks whether large elections aggregate voters’ private information as if society had full information.
  • The result: the “wisdom of crowds” is not automatic. Voting systems and information environments have to be designed to aggregate knowledge well.
Angela Crema — Education, labor, empirical IO, peer effects, and discrimination

EducationLaborEmpirical IO

  • Works on economics of education, labor economics, and empirical industrial organization.
  • Research on peer effects in college applications asks how high school peers influence college-application behavior.
  • Research on motion pictures uses machine learning to examine racial identity, selection, revenue, and producer screening.
Gaston Chaumont — Macroeconomics, investor sentiment, and self-fulfilling beliefs

MacroeconomicsFinancial Markets

  • Studies macroeconomics and the power of investor sentiment.
  • Work inspired by GameStop asks whether a sudden belief in a struggling company’s future can actually improve its survival odds.
  • This is a way to think about sunspots, self-fulfilling prophecies, and financial-market narratives that can move real outcomes.
Asen Kochov — Decision theory, uncertainty, and unknown unknowns

TheoryDecision Theory

  • Develops tools for modeling decisions under uncertainty in ways closer to how real people face them.
  • Research formalizes how to make choices when you cannot even list all possible future states.
  • This is especially relevant for students choosing majors and careers in a world shaped by AI, climate change, trade, and political instability.
