Letters of Recommendation

A recommendation letter is not a favor, a formality, or a credentialing ritual. It is a serious statement of judgment, credibility, and trust.

A recommendation letter is a serious act

A real letter says: I have seen this student work, and I am willing to put my name behind what I saw.

That is not paperwork. That is judgment. It is a public act of trust. I am telling a reader that my experience with you is strong enough, specific enough, and honest enough to matter.

Because of that, I will not write decorative letters. I will not write vague praise. I will not inflate ordinary performance into excellence. I will not make claims about your character, work ethic, judgment, or promise because those claims would be convenient for you.

If I write, I write seriously. If I cannot write seriously, I should not write.

Before you ask

Do I know you?A transcript reports grades. A letter requires knowledge of your work, judgment, seriousness, and character.
Have I seen evidence?I need work, conduct, writing, research, TA performance, office-hour discussion, or sustained intellectual engagement I can actually point to.
Is this serious?Not every application, campus role, internal box, or institutional hoop deserves a full faculty recommendation.

What My Letters Can Do Well

The best letters come from a real match between student, professor, evidence, and opportunity.

I can write best for students whose work I have actually seen

I am best positioned to write for students who have taken serious economics courses with me, written substantial work, participated thoughtfully in office hours or discussion, worked as TAs or research assistants, or shown unusual independence, judgment, resilience, seriousness, or intellectual courage.

Observed WorkSpecific Evidence

I can speak to how you think

My strongest letters are not about whether you were nice, polite, or present. They are about how you think, argue, recover, revise, write, and work when the answer is not obvious and the praise is not immediate.

JudgmentTenacity

I am useful when the reader needs a real assessment

If the opportunity needs someone to explain how you reason, work, improve, and take responsibility for serious intellectual tasks, I may be a good fit. If it only needs someone to certify that you are pleasant or in good standing, I am probably not the best use of the letter slot.

Matching Problem

I will not write ornamental letters

A decorative letter helps nobody. Readers know when a letter is thin. A weak, vague, or inflated letter does not merely fail to help. It tells the reader the recommender did not have much to say.

No OrnamentNo Inflation

Character, Evidence, and Fit

Grades matter. They are not the whole story. Serious letters live beyond the transcript.

What serious letters try to document

Character matters a lot more than your GPA. Recent research identifies broad occupational personality requirements such as intellectual tenacity and social adjustment: persistence, initiative, analytical thinking, independence, emotional regulation, cooperation, concern for others, and stress tolerance. These are the kinds of traits serious letters try to document when there is real evidence for them.

Evidence is the difference between praise and credibility

“Excellent student” is cheap if it floats free of evidence. “I watched this student do this hard thing, under these conditions, in this way” is different. That is what makes a letter believable.

Fit matters both ways

You are choosing a recommender. I am deciding whether I can serve as one honestly. This is a matching problem. A bad match produces a bad letter. I am not going to write you a bad letter.

Recommendation Letter vs. Basic Reference

Not every request calls for the same kind of response.

A recommendation letter

A recommendation letter is a written argument on your behalf. It requires evidence, judgment, care, and time. It should add something a transcript, resume, or form cannot provide.

ArgumentEvidence

A basic reference

A basic reference is much simpler: confirming that you took a class, performed well, worked responsibly, or are in good standing. Some campus roles and routine applications need only that. If so, provide my contact information rather than asking for a full letter.

What Your Request Must Include

Do not send a vague “Can you write me a letter?” email. That is not a serious request.

1

What you are applying for

Include the opportunity name, deadline, purpose, and why it is worth a serious faculty letter.

2

Why you are asking me

Explain what I can truthfully say about you that another recommender could not say as well.

3

Two original work samples

All requests must include two samples of assignments you wrote for me, or correspondence you had with me. These must be original versions, not post-graded GPT-polished versions. I reserve the right to attach student work to the letter so readers can corroborate what I say.

4

Submission links, deadlines, and instructions

Include the correct links, deadlines, and submission instructions. If there are more than four separate websites, reduce the list before asking or explain why the situation is unusual.

5

Relevant context

Remind me of the courses, assignments, office-hour conversations, TA work, research work, or other evidence that should inform the letter. Do not assume I will remember every detail from a large class.

6

Enough lead time

I write letters in bits and chunks while thinking about your work. The more time I have, the better the letter can be. Last-minute requests are usually not possible.

When I Am Not the Right Recommender

This is not a punishment. It is a standard.

If I do not know you

If I do not know you, I cannot write a meaningful personal letter. At best I can describe the class and report how you performed. That is not much more than your transcript already does.

For routine non-academic box checking

I generally do not write full personal letters for residential life, Take Five, e5, study abroad, student organizations, tour guides, peer advising, and similar non-graduate-school or non-professional requests. If someone needs a basic academic reference, provide my contact information.

For institutional theater

Some opportunities do not merit a full faculty recommendation. If the letter is mostly credential inflation, a low-value hoop, or an attempt to make ordinary campus participation look more selective than it is, I may decline.

For slam-dunk cases where letters are not needed

If the opportunity does not genuinely require a substantive letter, or if the application is already decided by objective criteria, I may decline rather than manufacture unnecessary prose.

For ranking-motivated transfer requests

If your transfer request is motivated solely by rankings, I am not likely to write the letter. Transfer letters for students I do not know well will not be written and will be lower priority than letters for students committed to the academic mission here.

For claims I cannot honestly make

I will not opine on work ethic, character, cooperation, leadership, judgment, or resilience unless I have legitimate evidence. I will not write things merely because they would be nice for you to have in the file.

Website and Form Limits

The limit exists to protect the letter.

I am not going to make letters worse

A strong letter requires attention. I need to think about the student, the work, the opportunity, the evidence, the claims I can make, and the claims I cannot make.

If I am spending that time creating accounts, filling out duplicate rating grids, re-entering the same institutional details, and clicking through twelve portals, I am not spending that time thinking about your work. That makes the letter worse. I am not going to make letters worse.

For that reason, I will submit to a maximum of four total websites for a student unless we have discussed an unusual situation in advance. If a site asks for extensive survey responses that duplicate what is already in the letter, I may answer “N/A.” If a site asks me to judge issues I cannot legitimately assess, I will not make those claims.

Maximum Four WebsitesNo Repetitive SurveysQuality Control

Priority and Final Judgment

Not all requests are equal. I will treat them accordingly.

Economics majors and academically serious requests come first

Priority in letter writing will be given to Economics majors, serious graduate-school and professional opportunities, and requests connected to the academic mission of the University. Other requests may be declined even when they are not unreasonable in the abstract.

The standard

I will write only when I can write with evidence, specificity, and credibility.

If I say yes, I am taking the request seriously. If I say no, it is because the evidence, match, timing, opportunity, or administrative burden does not support the kind of letter a serious student should want written on their behalf.
