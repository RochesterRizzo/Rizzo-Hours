Letters of Recommendation

A recommendation letter is not a formality. It is a serious statement of judgment, credibility, and trust.

Letters are not box-checking paperwork

We have a very large student population, and there has been a marked increase in the number of entities asking for recommendation letters, reference forms, ratings, websites, surveys, and repeated credentialing rituals. I cannot treat all of this as an unlimited administrative obligation.

I take letters seriously because a good letter spends credibility. I will not write vague praise, inflate ordinary performance, or produce letters that are not deeply believable.

What a Real Letter Requires

These are the conditions under which a letter can actually help you.

I need real evidence

I must know you well enough to comment on your character, work ethic, reasoning skills, cooperation with others, ideas, critical thinking, independence, and follow-through. A transcript already reports grades. A useful letter must add something more.

EvidenceCredibility

A letter should add to your candidacy

If you do not think I will write you an outstanding letter, reconsider whom you are asking. “Having a letter” is not enough. Readers compare letters, and there is information in what a letter does not say.

Strong Letters Only

I do not work in scalars

Even when multiple students apply to the same program, I try to emphasize each student’s particular strengths. But letters are still written in a way that helps serious readers assess relative merits honestly.

JudgmentNo Inflation

Good faith matters

A letter request should come from a student who is acting in good faith, taking the opportunity seriously, and not merely trying to extract a credential from someone who happened to teach a large class.

No Box Checking

When I Will Not Write

This is meant to be clear before anyone wastes time.

If I do not know you

If I do not know you, at best I can summarize what my classes are like and how you performed in them. That is not much more information than your transcript already provides.

For non-academic box checking

I cannot write personal letters for residential life, Take Five, e5, study abroad, student organizations, tour guides, peer advising, and similar non-graduate-school or non-professional requests. If someone needs a basic academic reference, provide my contact information.

For bullshit opportunities

I will not write letters for opportunities where the letter is mostly institutional theater, credential inflation, or a low-value hoop that consumes serious faculty time without serving the academic mission.

For slam-dunk cases where letters are not needed

If the opportunity does not genuinely require a substantive letter, or if the application is already decided by objective criteria, I may decline rather than spend time manufacturing unnecessary prose.

For ranking-motivated transfer requests

If your transfer request is motivated solely by rankings, I am not likely to write the letter. Transfer letters for students I do not know well will not be written and will be lower priority than letters for students committed to the academic mission here.

For claims I cannot honestly make

I will not opine on work ethic, character, cooperation, leadership, or judgment unless I have legitimate evidence. I will not write things merely because they would be nice for you to have in the file.

The Four-Website Limit

Administrative friction now has to be limited sharply.

I will not spend hours navigating letter portals

An increasing number of students do not merely ask for a letter. They ask for letters to be sent to many separate entities, each with its own emails, website registrations, address fields, recommender profiles, repetitive surveys, Likert-scale evaluations, and upload procedures.

I write letters for up to fifty students per year, often across dozens of applications. From now on, I will submit to a maximum of four total websites for a student. If a site asks for extensive survey responses that duplicate what is already in the letter, I may answer “N/A.” If a site asks me to judge issues of character or work habits that I cannot legitimately assess, I will not return those claims.

What Your Request Must Include

Do not send a vague “Can you write me a letter?” email.

1

What you are applying for

Include the opportunity name, deadline, purpose, and why it is worth a serious letter.

2

Why you are asking me

Explain what I can truthfully say about you that another recommender could not say as well.

3

Two original work samples

All requests must include two samples of assignments you wrote for me, or correspondence you had with me. These must be original versions, not post-graded GPT-polished versions. I reserve the right to attach student work to the letter so readers can corroborate what I say.

4

Links and submission instructions

Include the correct links, deadlines, and instructions. If there are more than four separate websites, reduce the list before asking.

5

Enough lead time

I write letters in bits and chunks while thinking about your work. The more time I have, the better the letter can be. Last-minute requests are usually not possible.

Priority

Time is limited, so priority has to be explicit.

Economics majors and academically serious requests come first

Priority in letter writing will be given to Economics majors, serious graduate-school and professional opportunities, and requests connected to the academic mission of the University. Other requests may be declined even when they are not unreasonable in the abstract.

Character Matters

Grades are not the whole story.

Character, jobs, and success

Character matters a lot more than your GPA. Recent research identifies broad occupational personality requirements such as intellectual tenacity and social adjustment: persistence, initiative, analytical thinking, independence, emotional regulation, cooperation, concern for others, and stress tolerance. These are the kinds of traits serious letters try to document when there is real evidence for them.
