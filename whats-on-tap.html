What are we talking about this week?

"I Hope You are Doing Well." Do you? I submit that we stop using that term immediately. Updated Sundays. These are the ideas, articles, examples, and provocations that may shape office-hour conversations this week.

This Week’s Big Theme

The decline of small private colleges. Is this a buying opportunity? Should some of them close? What is actually being lost? And is this just demographics, or is something deeper happening in the university marketplace?

Higher Ed Institutions Markets Creative Destruction Political Economy

Main Questions

1. Small colleges: Are struggling private colleges victims of demographics, bad management, changing preferences, winner-take-all competition, or all of the above?
2. What is lost? If small colleges close, do we lose something socially valuable that large universities cannot easily replace?
3. Rules versus relationships: When institutions stop relying on impersonal rules and start depending on insider relationships, who benefits and who gets locked out?
4. Democracy and losing: What makes losing an election, a budget fight, a policy dispute, or an institutional battle survivable?
5. Good faith: What would a smart person who disagrees with you say?

Provocations / Things to Argue About

Democracy, rules, and losing

The word “democracy” gets tossed around carelessly. A more serious question is whether people on all sides believe losing is survivable under impersonal rules. If losing means your opponents can use institutions to destroy you, politics becomes total war.

The problem is not primarily one leader, party, or policy dispute. The problem is whether both sides still believe losing is survivable under rules that apply impersonally.

One-party jurisdictions

Think about localities or states where one faction dominates. Are elections enough to discipline power if the real rule for getting elected is acceptability to a powerful local coalition?

Legalization and regulation

“Legalize it” often means “create a taxable, regulated, inspectable category.” The Humboldt County shed story is useful for thinking about how legalization can expand administrative control rather than reduce it.

Grade inflation

What are the long-run costs of making students feel better in the short run? If grades stop communicating information, who pays?

Green mandates and implementation costs

Electric school buses sound clean in theory. But what should we do when operating costs are higher, reliability is lower, and the people bearing the risk are school districts and children?

AI and HARKing

AI may make scholarship faster. It may also make it easier to manufacture plausible explanations after results are known. Is this a productivity miracle, an epistemic disaster, or both?

Loose Threads

  • Cell phone bans: Who wants them, who benefits, and what problem are they actually trying to solve?
  • Nonprofit fraud and public contracting: When does compassion become a racket?
  • Data centers: Infrastructure, poor regions, energy politics, and fake concern for the working class.
  • Social science ideology: What would count as evidence that a discipline is ideologically homogeneous, and why would it matter?
  • Greenpeace and pipeline litigation: Protest, property rights, torts, and the cost of “dissent.”
  • Yachts and cow methane: What should we make of high-consumption elites funding anti-consumption policy agendas?

For Lunch Bunch / Nerd Club

These are the more speculative, weird, philosophical, or fun discussion items. They may not belong in class, but they are perfect for conversation.

Rule of engagement: The goal is not agreement. The goal is good-faith argument. Understand the strongest version of the argument before attacking it.
  • Is democracy a set of procedures, a culture of losing gracefully, or both?
  • Are elite institutions becoming more winner-take-all, or are we simply noticing the sorting more clearly?
  • When does regulation solve a real externality, and when does it become a tax-and-control machine?
  • What is the difference between corruption as individual moral failure and corruption as institutional design failure?
  • Can AI improve research without destroying trust in research?

How to Use This Page

You do not need to read everything. Pick one item, think seriously about it, and bring a question or objection to office hours.

The standard is not agreement. The standard is good faith: understand the strongest version of the argument before attacking it.
